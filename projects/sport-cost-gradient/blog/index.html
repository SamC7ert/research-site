Home » Research Projects » Income Inequality, Sport Cost, and Olympic Performance

Research Journal

7 min · Sam C. Syvertsen

This research did not begin with a hypothesis. It began with a question about Norway.

Why does Norway win so many golds?

Norway’s gold-to-total medal ratio at the Summer Olympics seemed unusually high for a small, egalitarian country. I wondered whether this was a pattern — whether equal societies produce winner types more efficiently. It would have been an interesting paradox: equality breeding dominance.

It wasn’t. The gold-to-silver ratio showed no systematic relationship with income inequality. The pattern was noise.

But the initial investigation — comparing country-level Olympic performance against economic and social indicators — revealed something else. Using data from seven Summer Games (Sydney 2000 through Paris 2024) and a top-six scoring system, income equality appeared to predict Olympic success per capita, even after controlling for GDP. The signal from income equality was stronger than that from gender equality, which was unexpected.

This looked like a publishable finding. It wasn’t.

The result that killed itself

Adding regional fixed effects — controls for geographic clusters like Scandinavia, East Asia, Latin America — made the Gini coefficient drop to zero. The inequality–performance correlation was a textbook case of Galton’s Problem: what looked causal was actually regional clustering. Scandinavian countries are both equal and good at sport, but equality may not be why they’re good.

This should have ended the project. But the regional heterogeneity told a more complicated story. In some regions, inequality correlated positively with performance. In others, negatively. The aggregate zero was hiding real variation — 7 of 10 regions showed positive effects, 3 showed negative. A true null shouldn’t produce that pattern.

Something was varying systematically. The question was what.

“Maybe certain sports are different than others”

The question that changed the project wasn’t from the literature. It came from a prompt I wrote to the AI:

“What we can do now is also see if there are certain sports that are different than others. Maybe regional differences are just a consequence of different regions having different sports and different sports are more or less dependent on Gini and gender equality. More or less expensive equipment. Need for specialized arenas.”

This was the origin of the sport-cost idea. Not a pre-specified hypothesis. Not from a journal article. Just an observation that some sports require expensive equipment and facilities, and maybe that matters.

A subsequent literature review revealed that Geloso and Kufenko (2021) had independently theorised exactly this mechanism — income inequality creates participation cost barriers in expensive sports — but lacked the data to test it. We had arrived at the same intuition from a completely different direction.

Session 03 classified 50 Olympic sports into four cost tiers and ran interactions. The results were immediate: income inequality’s negative effect on performance increased with sport cost. The interaction coefficient was significant (p = 0.03 in Period 1). And critically, these cost-dependent effects survived regional controls where the aggregate relationship had not. The cost mechanism explained what Galton’s Problem had hidden.

The U-shape surprise

With a rebuilt panel dataset for v0.91 — 71 countries, 47 sports, seven Games — we expected a clean monotonic gradient: inequality hurts more as sport cost rises.

Instead, the data showed a U-shape. The cheapest sports (Tier 1) showed a positive Gini effect, and the most expensive sports showed the expected negative effect. The middle tiers were ambiguous.

The sport-type decomposition explained why. Three types of sport follow three distinct logics:

Team sports (football, basketball, hockey) cluster in the cheap tier but have no household cost barrier. A country needs exactly one team per event, so institutional investment and population matter more than what a family can afford. Their positive Gini effect in Tier 1 was a confound, not a mechanism.

Duel sports (judo, fencing, wrestling, taekwondo) show no cost sensitivity whatsoever. Cultural and martial traditions dominate — Japan in judo, Korea in taekwondo, Cuba in boxing. Cost is irrelevant to who wins.

Individual sports (swimming, cycling, sailing, triathlon) — events where participation requires personal investment — show the clean cost gradient. Among upper-income countries competing in individual sports, the interaction of Gini with log equipment cost is significant (p = 0.012). This is where the mechanism lives.

Two channels, not one

Session 11 produced the paper’s central result. We had been treating cost as a single dimension, but separating household equipment cost (what a family pays for gear) from facility infrastructure cost (what it costs to build a pool or velodrome) revealed two cleanly independent mechanisms:

Channel 1: Inequality × equipment cost. Income inequality interacts with household equipment cost but not with facility cost. When families face cost barriers, unequal societies produce fewer participants in expensive-equipment sports.

Channel 2: GDP × facility cost. GDP per capita interacts with facility infrastructure cost but not with equipment cost. Wealthy countries build swimming pools and velodromes. Poor countries cannot, regardless of their inequality level.

The cross-terms were zero. Equipment cost didn’t interact with GDP. Facility cost didn’t interact with Gini. And when both channels were included simultaneously, the Gini gradient attenuated by only 8% — near-complete independence.

This wasn’t a refinement. It was a reconceptualisation. Inequality and wealth operate through completely different sport-cost dimensions.

Getting the costs right

The channel separation only works if the cost classifications are credible. Sessions 10 and 13 document a painstaking process of building four-component cost estimates for all 41 sports in the main sample: equipment, coaching, facility access, and competition fees.

The estimates drew on German club fee structures (Beitragsordnungen), sporting goods pricing, and biographical research on Paris 2024 medallists. A crucial discovery: equipment-only measures understate true participation barriers by 2–5×. Swimming’s total cost is dominated by pool access (42%), not by goggles and swimsuits. Rowing’s facility share is 47%.

One example of the biographical validation method: every Paris 2024 marathon swimming medallist came through competitive pool swimming clubs. Open water training only begins post-selection. This confirmed that marathon swimming requires swimming pool infrastructure (€25M), not lakeside access (€50K). The “marginal cost” framing would miss this entirely.

Session 14 upgraded facility costs from ordinal tiers to continuous euro estimates, using Central European construction data. The transition strengthened the GDP–facility channel while the Gini–equipment channel remained stable. Both specifications — marginal cost and max-component — are reported for transparency.

A principle that governed the entire process: classifications were finalised before running regressions. As noted in Session 14: “We cannot test first, that is cheating.”

Does it hold up?

The robustness analysis tested the cost gradient across three dimensions:

Time periods: The gradient is consistent between Period 1 (2000–2008) and Period 2 (2012–2024). This is not a recent phenomenon.

Regions: 8 of 10 regions show negative gradients. Latin America is the notable exception (positive gradient), possibly reflecting Brazil’s dominance in cheap team sports.

Estimation methods: OLS on scoring countries, Tobit with zero-scoring countries, and OLS with zeros all confirm the negative gradient. The finding is not an artifact of sample selection.

The paper

By v0.95, the analytical results were stable. The full specification dashboard shows all model variants and their coefficients.

The remaining work was structural: organising nine sections, writing formal prose, and deciding how transparent to be about the AI-assisted research process.

The answer to that last question: fully transparent. All 15 session transcripts are published alongside this journal as a methodological companion. The AI (Claude) wrote pipeline code, implemented regression specifications, and drafted text. The research questions, analytical judgments, and cost classifications came from the human researcher.

What this project found

Income inequality does not predict Olympic performance in the aggregate — but this null conceals a systematic gradient. In individual sports, inequality increasingly hurts performance as personal equipment cost rises. A separate, independent channel runs through national wealth and facility infrastructure. The two mechanisms operate through completely different cost dimensions with near-zero mutual attenuation.

The finding is robust across time, regions, estimation methods, and cost measurement approaches. It provides the first empirical test of the cost-barrier mechanism that Geloso and Kufenko theorised but could not evaluate.
