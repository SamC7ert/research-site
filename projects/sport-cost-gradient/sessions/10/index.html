Session: v0.93 Cost Reclassification — Pilot Sports#

Date: 2026-02-23

Summary#

Extended the pilot cost analysis of 8 sports to develop a comprehensive four-component cost breakdown (equipment, coaching, facility access, competition expenses) for all Olympic sports.

Pilot Results#

Total annual costs for the 8 pilot sports ranged from €1,400 (judo) to €10,500 (sailing). Facility costs emerged as a major hidden driver: swimming (42% of total cost) and rowing (47% of total cost). Equipment-only measures understate true cost barriers by 2–5× while preserving relative ordering.

Methodology#

Two-stage approach: first source costs through web research (German sports club data), then use well-sourced pilot sports as reference anchors for remaining sports through relative positioning. This provides defensible claims for academic publication.

Research Approach#

German-language searches for club fee structures (Beitragsordnung) proved most effective for sourcing. Combining broad industry surveys with specific club examples provided the most reliable cost estimates.

What the paper says we’re estimating:

“Full annual cost of producing a competitive-pathway athlete aged 12–16, regardless of who bears the cost.” Four components: equipment, coaching, facility access, domestic competition. Central European benchmark.

The ambiguities I see:

  1. “Regardless of who bears the cost” — this is the big one. In Germany, a swimming club charges €90-150/year membership. But the coaching those kids receive has a real resource cost — the coach’s time, the pool hours allocated. The paper says we want the total resource cost, not the household’s out-of-pocket cost. So for swimming in a German Verein model, do we impute the coaching cost at what it would cost if purchased privately? Or do we record what the household actually pays through the club system?

    The paper’s logic (Section 2.6) says we want the full production cost because the relative ordering of sports needs to be country-invariant. If we used out-of-pocket costs in the German system, swimming and athletics would be nearly free, tennis would be moderate, and equestrian would still be expensive — compressing the bottom of the distribution. If we use full resource cost, we get better separation across the range.

    But this creates a sourcing problem. Club coaching costs are opaque — they’re cross-subsidised by membership fees, municipality grants, and volunteerism. We can’t observe the “true” cost of coaching 5×/week competitive swimming in a German Verein because no one is billing it that way.

  2. What counts as “competitive pathway”? Is this the kid who trains at a regional club and competes at Landesmeisterschaften? Or the kid on a Bundesstützpunkt track? These are very different cost levels. The paper says “club/regional level” — so pre-national, but serious.

  3. Coaching component — The handover says “hours/week × rate × weeks.” For a sport like athletics where 12-16 year olds train 4-5×/week in a Verein with a mix of group and individual coaching, what hourly rate do we use? The Übungsleiter rate (€15-25/hr)? The licensed Trainer-B rate (€30-50/hr)?

My proposed resolution:

Estimate coaching at the cost of hiring a qualified coach at Central European market rates (not volunteer rates), applied to the training volume typical for the competitive pathway. This gives us the resource cost. For facility access, use what a club or individual would actually pay for the required facility time. For equipment and competition, these are directly observable market prices.

This means our estimates will be higher than what many Central European families actually pay (because clubs subsidise), but will correctly capture relative ordering. A sport that needs 10 hours/week of pool time with a coach is genuinely more resource-intensive than one that needs 3 hours on a public track.
