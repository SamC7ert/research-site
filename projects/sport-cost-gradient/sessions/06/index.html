The 93% Norway statistic

The figure comes from Tom Farrey’s reporting in the New York Times (2019), based on what Norwegian sports system leaders told him: 93% of children grow up playing organized sports, with low costs and a philosophy of “Joy of Sport for All.” More precisely, by age 25, about 93% of Norwegians have played an organized sport at some point — so it’s a cumulative lifetime-participation-by-age-25 figure, not a rate of current participation at any given moment. Academic sources confirm this: about 93% of Norwegian teenagers have at some point been members of a sports club (Seippel & Skille, 2019).

For the US, the national average is roughly 55% of youth ages 6–17 who played organized sports in a given year, and only about two-thirds of Americans report playing a sport before age 18. So the gap is real, though the metrics aren’t perfectly comparable — Norway’s 93% is cumulative (“ever participated”), while US figures are often annual snapshots.

Is there cross-country data on this?

Comparable cross-national data on organized sports participation is patchy. One study by MyProtein ranked 134 countries on “regular sports participation” (at least monthly), putting Taiwan at 81%, Norway and New Zealand at 79%, and Australia at 77%. EU-wide data shows only 38% of Europeans play sport at least weekly, while Finland shows a 71% regular participation rate. But definitions vary wildly between surveys — some count walking or gym attendance, others only club-based organized sport.

Does mass participation predict Olympic success?

This is where it gets more nuanced, and more relevant to your research. The main academic framework here is De Bosscher et al.’s SPLISS model (Sports Policy factors Leading to International Sporting Success), which identifies nine pillars of policy that predict elite sport success. Pillar 3 is specifically about “participation” — grassroots sport involvement as a base for talent identification. Their research found that over 50% of international success can be explained by just three macro variables: population, wealth (GDP/capita), and (former) communism.

The critical finding from the SPLISS work is that the relationship between mass participation and elite success is not straightforward. There’s ongoing debate about whether a “trickle-down” from elite success stimulates grassroots participation, and conversely whether broad participation feeds elite pipelines. The evidence for a direct causal link in either direction is mixed.

Relevance to your sport-cost framework

The Norway argument is essentially a supply-side talent-pool story: more kids playing → larger pool → more chance of identifying and developing elite athletes. This connects to your work in an interesting way. Norway’s system specifically keeps costs low and access wide, which is essentially eliminating the cost-barrier mechanism you’ve been modeling with the Gini interaction. Norway’s 93% participation rate and its egalitarian structure could be seen as the flipside of your finding — they’ve removed the income-inequality penalty by design.

But the Norway narrative has limitations as a general explanation for Olympic success. It works well for Norway (small, wealthy, egalitarian, strong sports infrastructure), but it doesn’t obviously explain why, say, Kenya or Jamaica punch above their weight despite lower participation infrastructure, or why the US with its lower participation still dominates absolute medal counts.

There’s no standardized cross-country panel dataset of organized sports participation rates covering your 2000–2024 window. There are two paths to making it more defensible:

Path 1: Ground it in observable, measurable criteria

You could build a scoring rubric with explicit dimensions, each scored on a defined scale. Something like:

If I’m a small country’s sports minister trying to win a gold medal, I’m thinking about:
