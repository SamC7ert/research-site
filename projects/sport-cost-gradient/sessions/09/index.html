Summary#

Extensive statistical analysis comparing “scoring-only” versus “with-zeros” specifications, followed by creation of a detailed paper outline and structural decisions.

Key Results#

  • Team sports: positive Gini coefficient only in scoring-only samples (driven by USA, Brazil, Argentina); disappears or reverses with zero-scoring countries included.
  • Duel sports: no systematic inequality relationship.
  • Individual sports: confirmed hypothesized cost gradient.

Structural Decisions#

  • Eight-section paper outline created.
  • Equipment cost tiers removed in favor of continuous specifications.
  • All gender analysis moved to separate Paper 2.
  • “Horse race” jargon replaced with “joint specification.”
  • Title settled: “How Income Distribution and Sports Participation Cost Shape Olympic Performance”

Yes. With continuous ln(cost) you’re asking the data to trace a precise slope through 24 scattered points. With tiers you’re asking a simpler question: is T1 > T2 > T3? That’s an ordered hypothesis, and you can test it with fewer degrees of freedom.

Now explain what Tobit is?

Claude#

Tobit is a regression model for situations where your dependent variable is censored at a boundary — in our case, zero. It was invented by James Tobin in 1958 for the problem of household expenditure on durable goods: many households spend exactly zero on a new car in any given year, but the ones that do spend vary widely.

The idea: there’s a latent (unobserved) variable y* that can take any value, positive or negative. But we only observe y = max(0, y*). A country with latent Olympic capacity of −50 in Equestrian and a country with latent capacity of −5 both show up as zero points. Standard OLS treats them identically. Tobit says they’re different — one is much further from scoring than the other — and estimates the model accounting for this.

Mechanically, Tobit maximises a likelihood that has two parts: for zero observations, the probability that y* ≤ 0 given the covariates; for positive observations, the normal density of y given y*. This produces coefficient estimates that are unbiased when the data-generating process matches the censoring assumption.

Why it matters for us: In our sport-level panel, roughly 70% of country-sport-period cells are zero. OLS with zeros pulls the regression line toward zero for everyone, underweighting the variation among scorers. OLS scoring-only throws away information about who doesn’t score. Tobit uses both.

The catch: Tobit assumes the same process determines whether you score and how much you score. That’s questionable for team sports where qualifying for the Olympics (a discrete institutional hurdle) is fundamentally different from performing well once you’re there. The zero-inflated models Csurilla uses are better for that — they have separate equations for the two processes.

For our paper: scoring-only OLS is defensible and transparent. Tobit would be a robustness check worth running in the final analysis round, primarily to show reviewers we’re aware of the censoring issue. If results are similar across OLS scoring-only and Tobit with zeros, that strengthens the finding. If they diverge, we have a discussion point.
