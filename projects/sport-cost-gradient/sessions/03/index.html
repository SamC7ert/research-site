Summary#

Complete end-to-end analysis testing two hypotheses about how inequality affects Olympic performance across different sport cost tiers. Built on Geloso & Kufenko (2021) who theorized that income inequality hurts Olympic performance through cost barriers but couldn’t find quality sport-cost data. Created a sport cost classification system categorizing 50 Olympic sports into four tiers (low, medium, high, very high).

Key Findings#

  • Income inequality (Gini) increasingly hurts performance as sport cost rises (β = -0.016, p = 0.03 for Period 1), confirming the Geloso theory.
  • Gender equality (GGI) shows its strongest positive effects in expensive sports (β = 8.258, p = 0.009) — contrary to the original hypothesis that it would uniformly help across all sports through talent pool expansion.
  • Results resolve Kuppens & Pollet’s (2015) critique of Berdahl et al. by showing sport-cost mechanisms survive regional controls where aggregate relationships do not.

Outputs#

Six publication-quality figures, comprehensive regression tables, descriptive statistics, and a complete paper outline.

Summary of the literature, mapped to our research:

Bernard & Busse (2004) — The foundation. GDP (= population × GDP per capita) is the best single predictor of medal shares. They use a Tobit on medal shares 1960–1996 with population, GDP/capita, host, Soviet/planned dummies, and lagged medal share. Key for us: they treat all sports as homogeneous and don’t consider inequality or gender equality at all. Our sport-cost decomposition directly addresses this — GDP matters differently depending on sport cost.

Krishna & Haglund (2008) — “Effective participation” theory. They argue GDP doesn’t capture enough; what matters is how many people effectively participate (know about, have access to sports). Their proxy is radio ownership, which kills GDP per capita’s significance in an OLS. Urbanization also significant. This directly supports our cost-barrier story: radios = information access, but we add the material dimension — even informed people can’t do equestrian without a horse. Their effective participation concept is our sport-access channel given a formal mechanism.

Noland & Stahler (2014/2016) — Two papers (working paper and published version). Female participation and medaling driven by female education, female labor force participation, and communist bloc membership — not just income. GDP per capita becomes insignificant for the gender composition of delegations once these variables are included. This is the closest precursor to our GGI findings. But they don’t separate GGI from Gini or test the sport-cost interaction. We show GGI works through a different channel (talent pool) than Gini (cost barrier), and that GGI predicts medals even in cheap sports where income inequality is irrelevant.

Berdahl, Uhlmann & Bai (2015) / Bai, Uhlmann & Berdahl (2015) — The “win-win” papers. Gender equality (WEF Gender Gap Index) predicts medals for both men and women. Educational and economic gender equality are the strongest subindexes. They control for GDP, population, latitude, Gini. Key point: they show the Gini correlation with men’s medals is negative (r=−0.20), but don’t decompose by sport cost. Their Table 1 is useful — Gini correlates with men’s medals but not women’s. Our work explains why: Gini operates through cost barriers, which affect infrastructure-heavy sports where men historically dominate.

Kuppens & Pollet (2015) — The critique. They argue: (1) control for GDP per capita not GDP, (2) control for world regions because countries aren’t independent (Galton’s problem). When they do both, gender equality effects disappear or reverse for men. Within-region correlations between gender gap and medals are near zero. We need to address this directly. Our region controls partially do this. More importantly, our sport-cost decomposition provides a mechanism — the ecological fallacy critique weakens when you can point to a concrete channel (cost barriers for Gini, talent pool for GGI) rather than a raw country-level correlation.

Geloso & Kufenko (2021) — Inequality and institutions. They find Gini negatively affects medals per capita, but only in countries with low economic freedom (below a threshold of ~7.1 on the EFW index). In economically free countries, property rights allow athletes to capture returns from winning, offsetting inequality’s cost barrier. They use Rio 2016 only, cross-sectional. Their limitation (which they acknowledge explicitly): “we attempted to create a ranking of the costs of the different disciplines but the sources we found were of low quality.” This is exactly what we do. They couldn’t disaggregate by sport cost — we can and we show inequality’s effect is sport-cost-specific, not institution-conditional. We provide the complementary mechanism they were looking for.

Rewilak (2021) — The “non-determinants” paper. Uses Mundlak correction (quasi-fixed effects) on panel Tobit, 1996–2016. Once country fixed effects are included, GDP per capita becomes insignificant. Only population and host effect survive. Gender equality (secondary school enrollment ratio) also dies. The Mundlak country means absorb everything. Key implication for us: the time-invariant stuff that absorbs GDP is exactly what we’re trying to explain — sporting tradition, infrastructure, cultural preferences for certain sports. Our sport-cost decomposition opens that black box. Fixed effects say “something about this country makes it good at Olympics” — we say “it’s because rich countries have built expensive sporting infrastructure over decades.”

Csurilla & Fertő (2024) — Most recent methodological contribution. Sport-level data, 1996–2021, zero-inflated beta regression. They separate probability of winning any medal from how many medals. GDP matters for the first medal but not the quantity beyond that. Population matters throughout. Superpower dummies (US, China, Russia) are important. Sport-level fixed effects matter. Confirms that sport-level heterogeneity is real and that aggregate models miss important variation — which is exactly our argument.

Forrest et al. (2017) — Listed but we only have a catalog page. They disaggregate Bernard & Busse by sport and find GDP effects vary by sport. This is our most direct predecessor. We add inequality × sport-cost interaction, which they don’t do.

How this maps to our positioning:

Our contribution relative to the literature is threefold:

  1. Sport-cost decomposition of inequality (new): Nobody has shown that Gini’s effect is confined to expensive sports. Geloso wanted to do this but couldn’t. Forrest et al. showed GDP varies by sport but didn’t add inequality.

  2. Separating Gini from GGI mechanisms (new): Noland & Stahler and Berdahl et al. showed gender equality matters. We show it works through a different channel than income inequality — talent-pool expansion vs. cost barriers — and that these are complementary, not competing.

  3. Addressing the ecological fallacy through mechanism (addresses Kuppens & Pollet): Rather than arguing about whether region controls are appropriate, we provide concrete mechanisms that make the ecological fallacy less damaging. When Gini predicts medals only in expensive sports and not cheap ones, the mechanism is concrete and testable.
