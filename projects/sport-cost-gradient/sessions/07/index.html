Now I have a good picture of the methodological landscape. Let me think through this properly.

The literature reveals a clear methodological spectrum:

What the field uses:

  • Bernard & Busse (2004): Panel Tobit on medal shares (canonical)
  • Rewilak (2021): Panel Tobit, plus hurdle model and Heckman selection as sensitivity
  • Geloso & Kufenko (2021): Threshold model + hurdle (gamma + logit), cross-section only (Rio 2016)
  • Csurilla (2024): Zero-inflated beta regression at sport level, most methodologically sophisticated
  • Noland & Stahler (2016): Tobit on medal shares
  • Forrest et al. (2017): Tobit sport-by-sport

The key methodological tension for our paper:

Our DV is total points (6-5-4 scoring) at Country × Sport × Period level. This creates a pile of zeros — most countries score zero in most sports. The question is whether the zeros and the positive values are governed by the same process.

Here’s my read on what makes sense and what doesn’t:

OLS on levels is the simplest and most transparent. The zeros are genuine zeros (the country really didn’t place top-6 in that sport). With sport FE, period FE, and clustered SEs, this is defensible and easy to interpret. The interaction coefficient (Gini × Cost) has a direct interpretation: “one unit more Gini costs X more points in high-cost sports than low-cost sports.” JIE readers will appreciate interpretability.

Panel Tobit is the canonical choice in this literature and reviewers will expect to see it. But Tobit assumes censoring — that there’s a latent desire to win medals that’s censored at zero. For Country × Sport, that’s actually a stretch. North Korea doesn’t have latent equestrian performance censored at zero; it has zero equestrian performance. Csurilla (2024) makes this critique explicitly.

Hurdle / two-part model (à la Geloso & Kufenko) separates the extensive margin (does a country participate/score at all in this sport?) from the intensive margin (how much does it score, given it scores?). This is conceptually cleaner and actually interesting for us — the cost barrier might operate differently on entry vs. depth.

Zero-inflated beta (Csurilla) is the methodological frontier but requires a share-based DV, adds complexity, and the interaction terms become harder to interpret.

Poisson/negative binomial treats points as count-like data. Handles zeros naturally, log-link gives multiplicative interpretation. But our DV isn’t strictly a count — it’s a score from a weighted scheme.

My recommendation for the paper structure:

  1. Main specification: OLS on levels with sport FE, period FE, region controls, clustered SEs by country. Transparent, interpretable, and the interaction term is the star. This is what JIE readers want to see.

  2. Robustness: Tobit because the literature expects it, and a hurdle model because it’s conceptually interesting (does cost barrier operate on extensive vs. intensive margin differently?).

  3. Skip zero-inflated beta — it’s overkill for our research question and obscures the interaction coefficients.

The bigger methodological question isn’t the estimator — it’s how to handle the non-random regional Gini signs that motivate the whole paper. That’s where the detective work matters. The sequence is:

  • Run aggregate regression, show Gini significant
  • Add region dummies, show Gini collapses
  • Extract within-region Gini coefficients, show they’re non-randomly signed (some regions show positive Gini effects, others negative)
  • Demonstrate this isn’t chance — perhaps a permutation test or F-test on the joint significance of region-specific Gini slopes
  • Then introduce sport cost as the explanation for why the signs vary

That motivating step (showing non-randomness of regional signs) might need its own methodological care. A formal test of “are these region-specific Gini coefficients more dispersed than chance?” would strengthen the paper considerably. A Wald test on region × Gini interactions in the aggregate model would do it.

What’s your view? Does OLS-main with Tobit/hurdle robustness match your instinct? And do you want to formalize the non-randomness test, or keep it descriptive?

Human#

Tell me a little more on how the communist control work.

Claude#

The communist dummy is a single binary variable (1/0) that enters the regression alongside Gini. What it does mechanically is shift the intercept for all 27 communist-legacy countries — it says “these countries score X points more (or less) than non-communist countries, holding GDP, population, Gini, and period equal.”

In the aggregate model (T2 Model 3), the communist coefficient is +310 points (p = 0.003). Post-communist countries systematically outperform what their GDP would predict, because they inherited Soviet-era sport infrastructure, training systems, and cultural emphasis on athletic achievement. That premium is large — roughly 310 points over a period, which is more than most countries score in total.

The interaction with Gini is indirect but powerful. Post-communist countries tend to be both more equal (lower Gini, typically 25-35) and better at the Olympics. Without the communist dummy, the regression sees “low Gini → lots of points” and attributes that to equality helping performance. Once you add the dummy, the model separates the effect: “these countries are good because they’re post-communist, and they also happen to be equal.” The Gini coefficient collapses because the apparent inequality penalty was really a communist-legacy premium in disguise.

In the within-region regressions (T4), the communist control matters for regions that contain both communist and non-communist countries. Eastern Europe is all communist so the dummy has no within-region variation there — the coefficient can’t change. But in Eastern Asia, you have China, North Korea, and Mongolia (communist) alongside Japan, South Korea, and Taiwan (non-communist). Without the dummy, Gini picks up the fact that the communist trio happens to have higher inequality than Japan/South Korea but also strong Olympic systems. With the dummy, the model attributes their performance to the communist legacy rather than their Gini levels, and the Gini coefficient flips from +40 to −61. Similar story in Southern Europe (Croatia, Slovenia are communist legacy) and Northern Europe (Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania).

The regions that don’t change at all between the two specs — Anglosphere, Western Europe, South & SE Asia — are the ones with zero or near-zero communist countries. The dummy has nothing to absorb there.

The deeper point is that the communist variable illustrates how fragile these within-region estimates are. A single binary control reshuffles the signs dramatically. With 4-10 countries per region and multiple confounds, trying to isolate a Gini effect at the regional level is not viable. That’s the motivation for moving to sport-cost decomposition — it exploits within-country variation across 41 sports rather than trying to compare a handful of countries within a region.
