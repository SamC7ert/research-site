The problem with replicating this for facilities#

Facility costs are structurally different from household equipment costs:

  • No Tinongo equivalent. Tinongo reports what families pay, not what facilities cost to build. There’s no standardised German portal for construction costs by sport type.
  • Fewer data points per sport. A sport might have 3-5 well-documented facility builds worldwide, vs. hundreds of Verein fee schedules for equipment.
  • Wider cost ranges. A “50m pool” can cost €15M or €85M depending on specification, site, and country. Central European benchmarking narrows this but doesn’t eliminate it.
  • The cheap end is hard to price. What does “a hall with mats” cost? Wrestling, judo, boxing all need essentially the same thing — a general-purpose sports hall. The cost isn’t sport-specific, it’s generic municipal infrastructure.

Key methodological decision to make before starting#

What counts as “the facility” for shared-infrastructure sports?

This matters a lot for the regression interpretation. Options:

  1. Full facility cost regardless of sharing. A 50m pool costs €25M whether it serves swimming, diving, water polo, and artistic swimming or just swimming. This prices the national infrastructure investment needed.
  2. Per-sport share of multi-sport facilities. If a pool serves 4 aquatic sports, each gets €6M. This avoids double-counting but introduces arbitrary allocation.
  3. Sport-specific marginal cost. Water polo needs “a deep pool” — what’s the marginal cost of depth beyond a standard pool? Diving needs platforms and dry-land — what do those add?

The handover document’s definition — “the minimum facility a country needs to develop Olympic-competitive athletes in the sport” — points toward option 1. A country that wants to produce competitive swimmers must build a 50m pool regardless of whether divers also use it. That said, option 1 means swimming, diving, water polo, and artistic swimming all get the same facility cost (~€25-30M), which may be too coarse.

My recommendation: Use option 1 (full facility cost) as default, with option 3 (marginal additions) only where there’s a meaningful sport-specific add-on cost (e.g., diving platforms + dry-land facility on top of the pool). This keeps it simple and defensible. The alternative — arbitrary per-sport allocation — introduces more researcher degrees of freedom than it removes.

Human#

What does water based mean

Claude#

The pitch surface has no sand infill — it’s a very dense, short-pile carpet that needs to be wetted with sprinklers before and during play. The water layer makes the ball run fast and true, which is what elite hockey requires.

There are three types of hockey synthetic turf:

  • Sand-filled/sand-dressed — cheaper, lower performance, fine for club/school level. FIH “National” standard.
  • Water-based — the elite surface. FIH “Global” standard. Required for Olympics, World Cups, major international events. Needs an irrigation system with sprinkler heads and water storage/recycling.
  • Dry turf (emerging) — FIH is trying to develop a water-free alternative for environmental reasons. Not yet certified at the highest level.

The irrigation system is what drives the cost difference. A sand-dressed pitch might cost £500–600K while a water-based one costs £935K+ per the Sport England data. The water system adds the tank, pumps, piping, sprinkler heads, and ideally a recycling system.

For the classification: our definition says “minimum facility to develop Olympic-competitive athletes.” Olympic hockey is played on water-based surfaces. Athletes need to train on the surface they compete on. So water-based is the correct specification — a country training its athletes only on sand-dressed pitches would be at a competitive disadvantage.

The national infrastructure barrier. What a country must invest to produce Olympic finalists in that sport.

A country that wants competitive swimmers must build a 50m pool. A country that wants track cyclists must build a velodrome. These are expensive, single-purpose investments that poor countries often cannot make. That’s the mechanism — GDP interacts with facility cost because richer countries can afford the infrastructure.

A water-based hockey pitch exists because someone decided to invest in hockey. A Sporthalle exists because a municipality needed a school gym.

That means combat sports, team ball sports, and other generic-hall users should be priced at their sport-specific increment — mats, equipment, minor adaptations — not the building. The old €0.1–0.5M range for wrestling was actually closer to the right concept than my €2M Sporthalle estimate.

Human#

What would someone criticizing this decision say?

Claude#

The obvious critique: you’re assuming the Sporthalle already exists. That’s true in Germany, Netherlands, Austria — countries with dense municipal sports infrastructure. It’s not true in much of Africa, South Asia, Central America. A country like Ethiopia or Bangladesh doesn’t have Sporthallen on every corner. If they want to develop wrestlers, they need to build something.

So by pricing at the sport-specific increment, you’re implicitly benchmarking against a country that already has general-purpose sports infrastructure. You’re measuring the marginal cost of adding a sport, not the total cost of building a sports system from scratch.

The counterargument to the critique: that’s exactly what the regression specification does anyway. GDP is already in the model as a main effect. GDP captures whether a country has basic infrastructure — roads, buildings, schools with gyms. The interaction term GDP × facility_cost is asking: conditional on a country’s wealth level, does it matter more for expensive-facility sports than cheap ones? If every sport gets a €2M Sporthalle added, the interaction term can’t distinguish between “needs a velodrome” and “needs a hall with mats.”

A second critic might say: you’re being inconsistent. A hockey pitch is also “general sports infrastructure” in the Netherlands — every school and club has one. You price it at €1.1M because from a global perspective it’s specialist. But a Sporthalle is also specialist from the perspective of a country that doesn’t have one. Where do you draw the line?

The response: the line is whether the facility is sport-specific in its design and construction. A 50m pool can only be a pool. A water-based hockey pitch can only be a hockey pitch. A velodrome can only be a velodrome. A Sporthalle serves 15 different sports interchangeably. The specificity of the investment is what creates the barrier, not the existence of a building.

A third critic: by pricing generic-hall sports near zero, you’re saying facility cost doesn’t matter for these sports. But it does — a country with no indoor facilities at all can’t develop indoor sports. You’re confounding “low sport-specific cost” with “no facility barrier.”

The honest answer to that critic: yes, and GDP as a main effect should capture that. The facility cost variable is designed to capture the sport-specific gradient on top of general infrastructure, not the baseline infrastructure itself.
