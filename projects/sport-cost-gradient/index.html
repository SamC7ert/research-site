Income Inequality, Sport Cost, and Olympic Performance

Does income inequality affect Olympic performance differently depending on what it costs to participate in a sport?

Working Paper#

Target journal: Journal of Institutional Economics

Status: Draft v0.95 (February 2026)

Abstract#

Geloso and Kufenko (2021) hypothesised that income inequality affects Olympic performance through sport-specific cost barriers, but could not test this due to the absence of reliable sport-cost data. We construct a two-dimensional cost classification – household equipment cost and training facility cost – for 41 Olympic sports and test whether inequality’s effect varies with cost, using a panel of 71 countries across seven Summer Games (2000–2024), scoring the top six finishers per event.

The aggregate Gini–performance relationship is zero after regional controls – a textbook instance of Galton’s Problem. But this null conceals a systematic gradient: inequality increasingly hurts performance as personal participation cost rises.

The two cost dimensions capture distinct mechanisms. Income inequality interacts with household equipment cost but not with facility cost. GDP per capita interacts with facility cost but not with equipment cost. In a joint specification, both channels survive with near-zero mutual attenuation (8%). Inequality operates through household cost barriers; absolute wealth operates through infrastructure provision.

Key Findings#

  1. The Aggregate Puzzle (Galton’s Problem): The Gini–performance relationship is zero after regional controls. But this null hides real heterogeneity – 7 regions show positive effects, 3 negative.

  2. The Sport-Cost Gradient: Inequality’s effect varies systematically with sport cost. In individual sports among upper-income countries, the interaction of Gini with log equipment cost is significant (p = 0.012). Team and duel sports show no such gradient.

  3. Two Distinct Channels: GDP operates through facility infrastructure cost. Gini operates through household equipment cost. These are independent mechanisms with near-zero mutual attenuation.

  4. Country Stories: Some countries systematically overperform in expensive sports (Great Britain, New Zealand, South Korea) while others show the reverse (United States, Norway, Hungary) – patterns unexplained by income, population, or inequality.
